## Blockchain Basics

📂 **Blockchain:** A shared digital ledger that records transactions across many computers, making it transparent, secure, and free from central control.  
📂 **Web3:** The new era of the internet built on blockchains—where users can _read, write,_ and _own_ their data, digital assets, and online identities.  
📂 **Ownership Economy:** A system powered by Web3 that lets people earn, hold, and benefit from the value they help create—rather than platforms keeping it all.  
📂 **Decentralization:** The idea of spreading power and control across a network instead of one company or authority.  
📂 **Distributed Ledger:** A record of transactions that exists across many computers instead of one database—ensuring transparency and resilience.

## Crypto, Tokens & Assets

📂 **Cryptocurrency:** Digital money that moves through blockchain networks, allowing people to send, receive, and store value without banks.  
📂 **Token:** A digital representation of value or rights on a blockchain—used for currency, access, or participation in a project or community.  
📂 **NFT (Non-Fungible Token):** A one-of-a-kind digital item—like art, collectibles, or in-game assets—verified and owned through blockchain.  
📂 **Fungible Token:** A token that can be exchanged one-to-one with another of the same kind—like ETH or USDC.  
📂 **Digital Asset:** Anything valuable stored and owned online, such as tokens, NFTs, or other blockchain-based items.

## How It Works

📂 **Ethereum:** A leading blockchain platform where people can create decentralized apps (dApps) and smart contracts using its native token, ETH.  
📂 **Smart Contract:** Code that automatically runs when certain conditions are met—no banks, lawyers, or middlemen needed.  
📂 **Proof of Stake (PoS):** A consensus method where validators lock up (stake) their tokens to help verify transactions and secure the network, earning rewards in return.  
📂 **Consensus Mechanism:** The process that helps all blockchain nodes agree on the same version of the ledger, keeping data accurate and trusted.  
📂 **Validator:** A person or computer node that confirms transactions and adds new blocks to the blockchain, earning rewards for their work.  
📂 **Node:** A computer that stores and updates a copy of the blockchain, helping keep it secure and synchronized.

## Using Web3

📂 **DEX (Decentralized Exchange):** A platform that lets users trade crypto directly with one another through smart contracts, not a company.  
📂 **Exchange:** A marketplace where users buy, sell, or swap crypto. Some are centralized (run by a company), others decentralized (run by code).  
📂 **On-Ramp:** A tool or service (like Coinbase or Base’s bridge) where you can buy crypto with regular money.  
📂 **Wallet:** A digital tool (mobile app or browser extension) for storing, sending, and managing your crypto and NFTs safely.  
📂 **Gas Fee:** A small payment in crypto to process a blockchain action, like sending tokens or minting an NFT.

📂 **Wallet:** A software application or physical device that lets you send, receive, and manage cryptocurrencies — and access Web3 apps.  
📂 **Web3 Wallet:** A self-owned wallet that gives you full control of your assets and connects you directly to decentralized applications (dApps).  
📂 **Custodial Wallet:** A wallet managed by a third party (like an exchange or app) that stores and controls your crypto for you.  
📂 **Non-Custodial Wallet:** A self-custody wallet where only you have access to your private keys and funds.  
📂 **Hot Wallet:** A wallet connected to the internet (like Base App, MetaMask, or Brave Wallet). Easy to use but more exposed to hacks.  
📂 **Cold Wallet:** An offline wallet (like Ledger or Trezor), ideal for keeping large amounts of crypto safe long-term.

## Advanced Wallet Features

📂 **Account Abstraction:** A blockchain upgrade that makes wallets easier to use — enabling features like gasless transactions and password recovery.  
📂 **Smart Accounts:** Wallets controlled by code instead of one private key, allowing flexible security like two-factor approvals.  
📂 **Sponsored Transaction:** When another user, app, or wallet provider pays your gas fee so you can transact for free.

## Security Concepts

📂 **Phishing:** A scam that impersonates trusted sites or people to steal wallet info or seed phrases.  
📂 **Revoke Access:** Removing a dApp’s permission to access your tokens (using tools like Revoke.cash).  
📂 **Social Recovery:** A safety feature that lets trusted contacts or backup devices help you recover your wallet.  
📂 **Scam NFT:** A suspicious NFT sent to your wallet — never click or interact; it could contain malicious links.

## quests
💭 **If I used MetaMask before, can I use the same wallet in Base or another app?**  
Yes. Your wallet is your seed phrase, not the app.

MetaMask, Base app, Coinbase Wallet: they’re all just interfaces. If you import the same seed phrase, you’ll see the same wallet, same address, and same assets.

Just be very careful where you enter your seed phrase. That’s the most sensitive piece.

💭 **How do scammers “spoof” addresses if blockchain is supposed to be tamper-proof?**  
They’re not changing the blockchain. They’re tricking you before the transaction happens.

Common tactics:

- Copy/paste malware that swaps wallet addresses
    
- Addresses that look very similar
    
- “Address poisoning” where scam addresses appear in your history
    

The blockchain itself is still correct. The mistake happens at the interface level.

Rule of thumb: always double check the full wallet address before sending.

💭 **Are revoke apps like** [**revoke.cash**](http://revoke.cash/) **basically like freezing your credit? Do you have to connect your wallet?**  
That’s a decent mental model. They let you review and revoke token approvals you previously gave to apps or contracts. Yes, you connect your wallet to check and revoke permissions, but connecting alone is not the same as granting spending approval.
